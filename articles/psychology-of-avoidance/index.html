The Psychology of Why We
Avoid What We Want Most

On self-sabotage, the terror of getting what you wished for, and the quiet war we wage against our own desires.

12 min read ~2,800 words The Deep Dive

In 1852, a young man from Calcutta named Michael Madhusudan Datta did something that baffled the people who loved him. He had, by every measure, arrived. His English verse was being praised in the salons of Bengal. He was sharp, charming, fluent in Greek and Latin, beloved by the literary circles of Madras. And then, just as recognition was within reach, he fled to Europe, piled up debts he had no plan to repay, married twice, drank heavily, and spent the next decade ensuring that the career everyone said he was destined for would remain perpetually just out of reach.

He eventually came back and wrote Meghnad Badh Kavya, one of the great epics of Bengali literature. But the years of self-undoing are not a footnote in his life. They are the story.

When you read about Madhusudan now, the word that appears most often is "tragedy." But I am not sure that is the right word. Tragedy implies a force that strikes from outside. What Madhusudan did, what so many of us do in smaller and quieter ways, was from the inside. It was, in the strange grammar of the human mind, something closer to a choice.

There is a phenomenon that psychologists have been circling for decades without giving it a fully satisfying name. Some call it self-handicapping. Others call it self-sabotage. The philosopher Eugene Gendlin called the felt quality of it "the stuck place." The Bhagavad Gita calls it svadharmavipatti, the corruption of one's own purpose.

स्वधर्मविपत्ति Svadharmavipatti — the corruption of one's own purpose. From the Bhagavad Gita, the idea that the greatest failure is not external defeat but the abandonment of one's own path.

Whatever you call it, you have felt it. The novel you keep not writing. The difficult conversation you keep rehearsing but never having. The career change you have described in such vivid detail to so many friends that it almost feels like you have already made it. The relationship you have been "almost ready" for since 2019.

This is not laziness. Laziness does not care enough to be elaborate. And avoidance of the things we want most is always elaborate.

I. The Saboteur Has Good Intentions

In 1978, two social psychologists at the University of Pittsburgh, Steven Berglas and Edward Jones, ran a now-classic experiment. They gave a group of students a set of unsolvable problems and then told each of them they had somehow done well. Before the next test, participants were given the option to take either a performance-enhancing drug or a performance-inhibiting drug.

A striking number chose the drug that would hurt their performance.

Why? Because if they failed after taking the handicapping drug, they had an excuse. And if they succeeded despite it, they would look extraordinary. The drug was insurance against the one outcome they could not survive: trying their hardest and still failing.

Berglas and Jones called this self-handicapping. But the more interesting question is not what people did in a lab. It is why the strategy makes a strange kind of sense to the part of our mind that is always, quietly, doing risk management.

The saboteur is not trying to destroy you. It is trying to protect you from a verdict you are not sure you can live with.

Our deepest ambitions carry something heavier than ordinary desires. They carry our identity. When I say "I want to be a writer," I am not just describing a preference the way I might prefer one brand of chai over another. I am saying something about who I believe I am, or who I believe I could be. That belief is precious. And it is fragile.

As long as I have not really tried, the belief is safe. The moment I try, and try seriously, I put the belief at risk. The mind, which is very good at preserving things we love, finds a solution: don't try. Or try in a way that is just credible enough to feel like effort, but not committed enough to count as a genuine test.

The saboteur is not trying to destroy you. It is trying to protect you from a verdict you are not sure you can live with.

II. The Fear Beneath the Fear

There is a story that circulates in psychotherapy circles about a patient who came to his therapist week after week with a different problem. The job he was miserable in. The relationship that was not working. The city he wanted to leave. The therapist noticed that all of these problems had the same shape: the patient was extremely good at describing, in forensic detail, everything that stood between him and what he wanted. And he was equally good at finding new obstacles the moment the old ones were cleared.

One day the therapist asked, "What would happen if all of this actually worked out?"

The patient went quiet for a long time. Then he said, "I don't know who I would be."

This is the fear beneath the fear. Not failure. Identity dissolution.

We are narrative creatures. We build a self out of story, and that story has, at its center, a protagonist who is becoming. The becoming is the point. "I am someone who is working toward something" is a surprisingly comfortable identity to occupy. It comes with built-in drama, built-in purpose, built-in community of other people who are also working toward things.

Arrival is destabilizing. If you get the thing, who are you now? The goal that organized your life is gone. The striving that gave you texture is over. The friends who bonded with you over shared aspiration may or may not follow you into this new place.

मंज़िल नहीं, सफर ही मकसद था। Manzil nahin, safar hi maqsad tha. — "The destination was never the point; the journey itself was the purpose." A common Urdu-Hindi expression, often used to describe how the process of seeking can become more meaningful than the goal itself.

There is wisdom in that Urdu sentiment. But there is also a trap. When the journey becomes a permanent residence, when the safar never ends because ending it would require facing what comes next, the beautiful idea has curdled into something else.

III. What Soichiro Honda Understood About Getting Stuck

In the late 1940s, Soichiro Honda was trying to build a small motorized bicycle for postwar Japan, where fuel was scarce and millions of people needed cheap transportation. He had a vision. He had no money and almost no resources. He wrote letters to bicycle shop owners across Japan, explaining his idea, asking for small investments. Fifteen thousand letters. About three thousand people responded with small donations of cash.

The detail that interests me is not the persistence. It is what Honda said, years later, about why he could keep going in those early years when almost everything was against him: he was genuinely uncertain whether it would work. The uncertainty, he suggested, was a feature, not a bug. It kept him nimble, observant, willing to revise.

Compare this to the kind of paralysis that strikes not at the beginning of a project but just before its completion. Screenwriters call it the "90% problem." Musicians know it as the album that is always "almost done." Entrepreneurs know it as the company that is perpetually "pre-launch." The closer you get to the thing being real, the more avoidance intensifies, because the closer you get, the less uncertainty there is to hide behind.

Uncertainty is, paradoxically, a comfort. Certainty, even the certainty of success, strips away the protection of "maybe."

IV. The Clever Disguises Avoidance Wears

The most insidious feature of self-sabotage is how virtuous it can appear. Here are some of its best costumes.

Perfectionism. The work is not ready. It needs one more revision. The idea has not fully crystallized. This sounds like high standards. Often it is a permanent deferral system. The philosopher Nassim Taleb writes, in The Bed of Procrustes (2010), about the difference between "optionality" and decision, suggesting that many people hoard options not because they are strategically flexible but because committing to a path means foreclosing on the fantasy of all the other paths.

Research and preparation. You need to read five more books before you start. You need to do a course first. You need to understand the landscape better. Sometimes this is true. More often it is the mind finding a way to stay in the safe territory of consuming without the exposure of producing.

Waiting for the right time. After the exams. After the wedding. After the kids are older. After the promotion. There is always an "after." The right time is a horizon that moves.

Helping everyone else first. This is the most emotionally sophisticated disguise. You are not avoiding your own work; you are being generous with your time and energy. You are a good friend, a present parent, a reliable colleague. And all of that may be genuinely true. And yet. And yet.

The writer Toni Morrison, in a 1977 interview with The Black Scholar, described a discipline she had to impose on herself as a working mother: she wrote before dawn, before the day's demands arrived. She was not waiting for space to open up. She created it, at the cost of sleep, because she understood that space does not open by itself for the things you are afraid of doing.

V. Why Getting Closer Makes It Worse

There is a concept in economics called "loss aversion," documented most systematically by the psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky in their research on prospect theory. The finding, simplified: the pain of losing something is roughly twice as powerful as the pleasure of gaining the equivalent thing.

What does this have to do with avoidance? Everything.

The moment a goal shifts from abstract to concrete, from "someday I will" to "this is actually happening," the potential loss calculus changes entirely. As long as the goal was distant, there was nothing to lose. Now, suddenly, there is. There is the draft that could be rejected. The relationship that could end. The business that could fail publicly. The painting that could be mediocre.

This is why proximity intensifies avoidance. The closer you get, the more real the potential loss becomes, and the more your mind mobilizes to protect you from it.

The dream is safe. Only the attempt is dangerous.

The Japanese have a concept worth borrowing here. Ma (間) refers to the meaningful pause, the pregnant space between things. In art and architecture, ma is not emptiness but potential. The space between notes that gives music its breath.

The problem is when ma becomes permanent. When the pause is no longer generative but protective. When the space between intention and action stops being a moment of gathering and becomes a place to live.

VI. What You Can Actually Do

This is not a self-help article, and I will not give you a five-step framework. But there are a few honest shifts in thinking that I find genuinely useful.

Name the specific fear. "I am afraid of failing" is almost never the real fear. The real fear is almost always more specific and more personal: "I am afraid that if I try seriously and it does not work, I will have to admit that this thing I have believed about myself is not true." Getting that specific is uncomfortable. It is also clarifying. Vague fears are enormous. Named fears have borders.

Decouple the attempt from the verdict. What you produce is not what you are. A failed manuscript is not evidence of a failed person. This sounds obvious, and it is genuinely hard, because we spent years building the identification between our ambitions and our selves. Uncoupling them is slow work. But it is the only work that makes sustained effort possible.

Watch for the elaborate quality of your avoidance. Simple avoidance is just distraction. When your avoidance becomes sophisticated, when you find yourself with excellent reasons, layered justifications, and a compelling narrative about why now is not the right time, you are probably in the presence of something important. The sophistication is a signal. It means the thing matters.

Madhusudan Datta eventually came home. He wrote his epic. He also died in poverty, having squandered much of what he could have built. His story is a reminder that the mind's talent for self-protection, left unchecked, has real costs. Not the abstract cost of "not reaching your potential," which is too large to feel, but the specific, daily cost of living at a slight remove from your own life.

There is a moment in the Mahabharata before the war begins, when Arjuna, looking at the armies arrayed against him, feels his bow slip from his fingers. Gandiva, the divine weapon, suddenly too heavy to hold. Scholars have read this as a crisis of duty. I read it, also, as something more ordinary: the moment when the goal you have moved toward your entire life becomes real, and the realness of it is more than you anticipated.

Krishna's answer to Arjuna is not a motivational speech. It is a philosophy: do what you are made to do, without attachment to the fruit of it. This is not indifference to outcomes. It is a different relationship with outcomes, one where the attempt and the result are separated enough that the fear of the result does not strangle the attempt.

Nishkama karma (निष्काम कर्म), action without the craving for result, is often taught as a spiritual ideal. But there is a very practical psychology underneath it. When you can do the thing without your entire identity riding on the outcome, the thing becomes possible. The bow stops being too heavy to lift.

You already know what you want. The question worth sitting with is this: what exactly would happen if you got it? And is the answer to that question the real reason you have not yet tried?
